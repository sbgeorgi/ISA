Pioneering Agrivoltaics Research

Since 2017, the University of Arizona has been at the forefront of agrivoltaics research, led by Dr. Greg Barron-Gafford's team at the Biosphere 2 facility. Our "solar garden" consists of a 21.6 kW array of solar panels elevated ~9 feet above crops, creating a living laboratory to study food, energy, and water dynamics in tandem.

Our early research garnered recognition as a Fast Company "World Changing Ideas" finalist for its innovative approach to sustainable agriculture and renewable energy integration.

Research Infrastructure

Our agrivoltaics research at Biosphere 2 utilizes cutting-edge infrastructure to study the interactions between solar energy production and agricultural cultivation:

  • 21.6 kW solar array elevated 9 feet above a 9 × 18 m agricultural plot
  • Equal-sized open-sun plot as a control for comparative studies
  • Drip irrigation systems and advanced monitoring equipment
  • On-site resources for measuring plant growth, soil moisture, microclimate, and solar panel performance [1] [2]

Biosphere 2's unique infrastructure, originally designed for closed-system ecological experiments, now provides an ideal setting for advancing agrivoltaic technologies.

Key Research Findings

Dr. Barron-Gafford's experiments at Biosphere 2 have revealed significant mutual benefits of agrivoltaics:

  • Increased Crop Yield: Initial trials showed vegetables like tomatoes, peppers, and herbs grown under solar panels produced more fruit compared to those in full sun [3].
  • Reduced Water Usage: Shaded plants experienced less heat stress, leading to reduced irrigation needs. Agrivoltaic systems can use less water to achieve comparable or superior crop yields [4].
  • Enhanced Solar Panel Efficiency: Vegetation cools solar panels through transpiration, resulting in a 5% increase in solar energy generation efficiency compared to panels over bare ground [3].
  • Mitigation of Heat Island Effect: Plants under solar arrays help dissipate heat through evapotranspiration, counteracting the "photovoltaic heat island" effect observed in large solar farms [5] [6].
  • No Flavor Difference: Taste-test studies have confirmed that produce grown under solar panels exhibits no discernible difference in flavor compared to open-sun crops [4].

These findings, published in Nature Sustainability [7], demonstrate the potential of agrivoltaics to optimize food, energy, and water resources, especially in arid environments.

Research Team and Publications

Our agrivoltaics research is driven by an interdisciplinary team led by Dr. Greg Barron-Gafford, including ecologists, geographers, and engineers. Key contributors at Biosphere 2's agrivoltaics lab include farm manager Isaiah Martin-Barnett, graduate students (e.g. Kai Lepley, Mariah Rogers), and collaborators in soil science and public policy [8].

Our seminal publications have become cornerstones in the field:

  • Barron-Gafford et al. (2019), Nature Sustainability [7]: Demonstrated the mutual benefits of agrivoltaics across the food-energy-water nexus.
  • Barron-Gafford et al. (2016), Scientific Reports [9]: Quantified the heat island effect of PV installations, laying the foundation for agrivoltaic cooling research.

Through these and other publications, the UArizona group has significantly advanced the scientific understanding of agrivoltaics' benefits in arid environments [10] [11].

Collaborations, Conferences, and Impact

UArizona's agrivoltaics initiative fosters extensive collaboration and has significantly raised the university's profile in this field. We are proud to partner with leading institutions and organizations:

  • USDA SCAPES Project: UArizona joined the $10 million USDA-funded project "Sustainably Co-locating Agricultural and Photovoltaic Electricity Systems" (SCAPES) in 2021 [12]. Dr. Barron-Gafford leads UArizona's $1.7 million scope as the physical science lead.
  • DOE FARMS Program: We received a $1.2 million grant from the U.S. Department of Energy under the "FARMS" program, partnering with AES Corporation to study agrivoltaics at an operational solar farm [13] [14].
  • Agrivoltaics World Conference: In 2024, Dr. Barron-Gafford co-chaired the Agrivoltaics World Conference (AWC) in Denver, the first time this global conference was held in the U.S. [15], alongside NREL's Jordan Macknick.

Our team actively participates in knowledge exchange, presenting research at international conferences and engaging with stakeholders to amplify the impact of Biosphere 2's agrivoltaic research. Our work has influenced regional stakeholders and inspired similar projects globally.

Future Directions and Funding Opportunities

The UArizona–Biosphere 2 agrivoltaics program is poised for continued growth and expansion. Our future research focuses on:

  • Scaling Up Agrivoltaics: New larger-scale experiments will investigate the scalability of agrivoltaic benefits and optimize designs for diverse crops and climates [16].
  • Best Practices Development: Insights from ongoing trials are informing best practices for panel height, orientation, and watering regimes, aimed at adoption by farmers and solar developers.
  • Securing Future Funding: Federal agencies (USDA, DOE, NSF) have prioritized agrivoltaics research, presenting strong funding opportunities [17] [18]. We are also exploring private sector partnerships to further invest in dual-use solar farms [19].

UArizona's interdisciplinary approach positions us as a leader in sustainability and climate resilience research, with potential for NGO and foundation support to implement agrivoltaic solutions for community development.
