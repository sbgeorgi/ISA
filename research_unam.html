Mexico's First Agrivoltaic Pilot Project: UNAM PASE

The National Autonomous University of Mexico (UNAM) pioneers sustainable food and energy co-production with the Topilejo PASE agrivoltaic plot.

Explore PASE Project

Background and Development of PASE

In 2022, the National Autonomous University of Mexico (UNAM) launched Mexico’s first agrivoltaic pilot project, PASE (Parcela Agrovoltaica Sostenible y Educacional), a milestone for Latin American agrivoltaics [32]. Located at UNAM’s veterinary field station in San Miguel Topilejo, PASE is designed to demonstrate sustainable food and solar energy co-production in Mexico’s climate.

PASE is a collaborative effort between UNAM’s Renewable Energy Institute (IER) and the Faculty of Veterinary and Zootechnical Medicine (FMVZ), funded by Mexico City’s Secretariat of Education, Science, Technology and Innovation (SECTEI) [33] [34]. This initiative reflects the strategic importance of agrivoltaics for protecting conservation land around Mexico City, generating clean energy, and supporting local agriculture [35].

Infrastructure and Design of PASE

The PASE agrivoltaic plot, covering 350 m², features a steel canopy supporting 72 photovoltaic panels several meters above ground [36] [37]. It includes:

  • Diversified garden with intercropped vegetables and forage plants.
  • Adjacent open-sky crop beds for control comparisons [38].
  • Drip irrigation system and extensive rainwater harvesting (145 m³ tank) [39] [40].
  • Strategic intercropping to study light and shade effects on different crops [41].
  • Solar-powered food dehydrators for post-harvest processing [42].

Sensors monitor microclimate effects, including soil moisture and temperature moderation, under the panels.

Research Goals and Early Findings

The Topilejo PASE project aims to evaluate sustainability dimensions of agrivoltaics. Key research goals include:

  • Assessing crop yield and quality under partial panel shading and intercropping arrangements [43].
  • Demonstrating water savings (up to 80% reduction) through reduced evapotranspiration and drip irrigation [44] [45].
  • Monitoring solar panel performance and efficiency enhancement due to vegetation cooling [46].
  • Evaluating rainwater catchment and solar dryers for holistic resource use and food security.

Early outcomes indicate significant water savings and efficient rainwater irrigation during dry seasons, with solar dryers enhancing food preservation and farm income.

Education, Outreach, and Collaboration

PASE, meaning "Sustainable and Educational Plot," emphasizes knowledge dissemination. It serves as a living classroom for students and farmers to demonstrate agrivoltaic practices.

  • Workshops and site visits to showcase "dual land use for solar power and agriculture" [47] [48].
  • Promoting agrivoltaics as a way for farmers to increase crop yields and income from solar electricity without land expansion [49] [50].
  • PASE is a collaborative hub within an international agrivoltaic research consortium [51].
  • Partners include University of Arizona, CNRS (France), UM6P (Morocco), and institutions in Israel and Kenya [52] [53].
  • PASE aligns with Mexico’s National Agrivoltaics Program, informing nationwide implementations [54] [55].

Future Prospects in Latin America

UNAM’s PASE project is a gateway for agrivoltaics in Latin America, providing a regional model with strong scaling opportunities. Potential future directions include:

  • Government funding (city, state, federal) for agrivoltaic systems in rural communities.
  • Expansion of SECTEI support to additional sites in Mexico City's conservation zone.
  • Investment from international development organizations and NGOs focused on water and renewable energy.
  • Private sector partnerships (solar developers, agribusinesses) under ESG frameworks.
  • UNAM's leadership in securing research funding from CONACYT and international climate funds.

Mexico's first agrivoltaic pilot is driving research and building momentum for broader agrivoltaic adoption across Latin America, promoting water-efficient farming and sustainable development [56] [57].
